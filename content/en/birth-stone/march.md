## About Aquamarine

### Gemological Profile

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Mineral Family | Beryl |
| Chemical Composition | Be₃Al₂Si₆O₁₈ (Beryllium Aluminum Silicate) |
| Hardness (Mohs) | 7.5 - 8 |
| Primary Sources | Brazil, Madagascar, Nigeria, Pakistan, Zambia |
| Colors | Pale blue to deep blue-green |

### Description

Aquamarine derives its name from the Latin words "aqua" meaning water and "marina" meaning sea, perfectly capturing the essence of this captivating gemstone. Its color ranges from pale, almost transparent blue to deeper shades of blue-green, reminiscent of tropical ocean waters on a clear day. The most prized specimens exhibit a pure, saturated blue without greenish undertones, though many gem enthusiasts appreciate the subtle sea-green hues that give each stone its unique character.

This gemstone belongs to the beryl family, making it a close relative of emerald. However, while emerald owes its green color to chromium and vanadium, aquamarine achieves its distinctive blue through trace amounts of iron within its crystal structure. The finest aquamarines possess exceptional clarity, often appearing almost flawless to the naked eye, which contributes to their remarkable brilliance and transparency.

Quality aquamarine is evaluated based on color saturation, clarity, and cut. Stones with deeper blue coloration command higher prices, though personal preference plays a significant role in selection. The crystal naturally forms in hexagonal columns, sometimes growing to impressive sizes that allow cutters to create substantial gems while maintaining excellent clarity throughout.

### Formation & Rarity

Aquamarine forms deep within the Earth's crust in pegmatite rocks, where the right combination of heat, pressure, and mineral-rich fluids allows beryl crystals to develop over millions of years. These geological conditions occur in relatively few locations worldwide, with Brazil's Minas Gerais region producing some of the finest specimens known to gemologists.

Unlike many gemstones that tend to form in small crystals, aquamarine can grow to remarkable sizes. The largest gem-quality aquamarine ever found, the Dom Pedro, weighed over 10,000 carats before cutting. While not as rare as some precious stones, high-quality aquamarine with intense blue color and exceptional clarity remains relatively uncommon and highly valued by collectors and jewelry enthusiasts alike.

## The History of Aquamarine

### Ancient Origins

Ancient mariners treasured aquamarine as a talisman of protection, believing the stone was a gift from the sea god Neptune. Sailors would carry aquamarine crystals on long voyages, trusting in the stone's legendary ability to calm waves and ensure safe passage across treacherous waters. This association with the sea permeated Mediterranean cultures, where the gem was thought to contain the very essence of ocean waters.

The Romans believed aquamarine could reconcile differences between enemies and restore harmony to relationships. They associated the stone with young love and believed that a gift of aquamarine could rekindle passion between married couples. Roman craftsmen fashioned the gem into intricate jewelry and talismans, often engraving images of sea creatures or Neptune himself into the polished surfaces.

In medieval Europe, aquamarine took on additional significance as a stone of foresight and prophecy. Practitioners of early fortune-telling arts used polished aquamarine crystals for scrying, believing the clear blue depths could reveal hidden truths and future events. The stone also gained a reputation for detecting poison—nobility would sometimes have drinking vessels carved from aquamarine or set aquamarine gems into goblets as a precaution against assassination.

### How It Became March's Birthstone

The tradition of birthstones traces back to the Breastplate of Aaron described in the Book of Exodus, which featured twelve gems representing the twelve tribes of Israel. Over centuries, these stones became associated with the twelve months of the year and the zodiac signs. The modern standardized list of birthstones was established in 1912 by the National Association of Jewelers in the United States, which officially designated aquamarine as March's birthstone.

The assignment of aquamarine to March holds particular significance as the month marks the transition from winter to spring in the Northern Hemisphere. Just as the warming season brings renewed hope and clarity after dark winter months, aquamarine's cool blue tones symbolize cleansing, renewal, and the promise of calmer days ahead. The stone's association with water also connects to March's frequent rains that nurture the earth for spring growth.

### Famous Aquamarine Specimens

The Dom Pedro aquamarine stands as the world's largest faceted aquamarine, now permanently displayed at the Smithsonian Institution. This magnificent gem, named after Brazil's first emperors, weighs 10,363 carats and measures nearly 14 inches tall. German gem artist Bernd Munsteiner transformed the raw crystal into a stunning obelisk-shaped masterpiece that captures and reflects light in mesmerizing patterns.

Queen Elizabeth II's collection includes several notable aquamarine pieces, including a stunning tiara and matching jewelry set gifted to her by the President and people of Brazil in 1953 for her coronation. The Brazilian Aquamarine Tiara features large aquamarine stones surrounded by diamonds, demonstrating the gem's royal appeal. Throughout history, many royal houses have prized aquamarine for its elegant beauty and symbolic connection to courage and tranquility.

## Wearing & Caring for Aquamarine

### Jewelry Guide

| Type | Suitability | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------|
| Rings | Good with care | Suitable for occasional wear; consider protective settings for daily use |
| Necklaces | Excellent | Ideal showcase for the stone's clarity and color |
| Earrings | Excellent | Protected position reduces risk of damage |
| Bracelets | Good with care | May be exposed to impacts; choose sturdy settings |

### Quality Factors

- **Color:** The most valued aquamarines display medium to medium-dark blue with high saturation. Avoid stones that appear gray or overly pale
- **Clarity:** Look for stones that are eye-clean with no visible inclusions. Aquamarine naturally tends toward high clarity
- **Cut:** Well-proportioned cuts maximize the stone's brilliance. Emerald and oval cuts are classic choices that complement the gem's natural beauty
- **Carat:** Larger stones often display richer color, as aquamarine's color can appear lighter in smaller sizes

### Care Instructions

Aquamarine's hardness of 7.5-8 on the Mohs scale makes it reasonably durable for jewelry, though it still requires proper care. Clean your aquamarine jewelry with warm soapy water and a soft brush, rinsing thoroughly and drying with a lint-free cloth. Ultrasonic cleaners are generally safe for aquamarine unless the stone contains unusual inclusions.

Store aquamarine separately from harder gemstones like diamonds and sapphires, which could scratch its surface. Avoid exposing the stone to extreme temperature changes or harsh chemicals. While aquamarine is relatively stable, prolonged exposure to intense heat or direct sunlight may affect some stones' color over time.

### Buying Tips

When purchasing aquamarine, prioritize color and clarity as the most important factors. Natural aquamarine with rich blue color commands premium prices, while lighter specimens offer more affordable options without sacrificing the stone's beauty and meaning. Most commercial aquamarine has been heat-treated to enhance its blue color—this is a standard, stable treatment widely accepted in the gem industry.

Synthetic aquamarine exists but is relatively uncommon in the market. Glass imitations and blue topaz are sometimes confused with aquamarine. For valuable purchases, request certification from a reputable gemological laboratory. Prices vary significantly based on quality, with fine specimens ranging from moderate to premium pricing depending on size, color intensity, and origin.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the birthstone for March?
The primary birthstone for March is aquamarine, symbolizing courage, serenity, and communication. This pale blue gemstone has been associated with March in the modern birthstone list since 1912.

### Are there alternative birthstones for March?
Yes, bloodstone (also called heliotrope) is sometimes considered a traditional alternative birthstone for March. This dark green stone with red spots carries its own rich symbolism related to vitality and protection.

### Is aquamarine suitable for everyday wear?
Aquamarine's hardness of 7.5-8 makes it reasonably suitable for regular wear, though some care is needed. For daily rings, choose protective settings that shield the stone from impacts. Necklaces and earrings face fewer risks and showcase aquamarine beautifully for everyday enjoyment.

### How can I tell if my aquamarine is real?
Genuine aquamarine feels cool to the touch and displays consistent color throughout. Look for natural inclusions under magnification—perfect clarity might indicate synthetic material or glass. For valuable pieces, seek certification from a gemological laboratory. Reputable jewelers can also help distinguish natural aquamarine from imitations or treated stones.

### What is the spiritual meaning of aquamarine?
Aquamarine is traditionally associated with courage, serenity, and clear communication. In spiritual traditions, it is believed to calm the mind, facilitate honest expression, and provide protection during travel. The stone's connection to water elements links it to emotional healing and intuitive awareness.
