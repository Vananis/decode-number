## About Carnation

### Botanical Profile

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Scientific Name | *Dianthus caryophyllus* |
| Family | Caryophyllaceae (Pink family) |
| Native Region | Mediterranean region |
| Blooming Season | Late spring to early fall |
| Colors | Pink, red, white, yellow, purple, and bi-colored varieties |

### Description

The carnation is one of the world's oldest cultivated flowers, with a history spanning over 2,000 years. Its name derives from the Greek word "dianthus," meaning "flower of the gods" or "divine flower," speaking to the reverence ancient cultures held for this bloom. The petals are distinctively ruffled and layered, creating a full, rounded appearance that has made carnations a staple in floral arrangements worldwide.

Carnations typically grow between 18 to 24 inches tall, with slender, gray-green stems and narrow leaves. The flowers themselves can reach up to 3 inches in diameter in larger varieties. What makes carnations particularly notable is their remarkable longevity—cut carnations can last up to three weeks with proper care, far outlasting many other cut flowers.

The fragrance of carnations is subtle yet distinctive, often described as spicy-sweet with hints of clove. This aromatic quality has made them popular not only in bouquets but also in perfumery throughout history. Their hardiness and ability to bloom across various conditions have contributed to their enduring popularity in gardens and commercial cultivation.

### History & Cultural Significance

According to Christian legend, carnations first appeared from the Virgin Mary's tears as she wept watching Jesus carry the cross, which is why pink carnations have become associated with a mother's undying love. This association has made carnations the traditional flower for Mother's Day in many countries.

In ancient Rome, carnations were used in ceremonial crowns and garlands, woven into elaborate headdresses for important occasions. The Greeks similarly employed them in ceremonial contexts, and the flower's association with celebration and honor has persisted through centuries. During the Renaissance, carnations appeared frequently in paintings, often symbolizing engagement or betrothal.

The carnation became associated with January through the Victorian tradition of assigning flowers to each month. Its ability to bloom even in cooler temperatures and its remarkable staying power made it a fitting symbol for the first month of the year—a time of new beginnings and enduring resolutions. Today, carnations remain among the most popular flowers worldwide, second only to roses in commercial production.

## The Meaning of Carnation

### Primary Meaning: Love, Fascination, Distinction

The carnation's rich symbolism has evolved over millennia, but its core meanings of love, fascination, and distinction have remained remarkably consistent. The layered petals are often thought to represent the many facets of love—from familial bonds to romantic passion to self-love. Each unfolding layer may reveal new depths, much like how relationships deepen over time.

Fascination as a meaning likely arose from the flower's captivating appearance and intoxicating scent. Throughout history, carnations have been used in love potions and worn to attract admirers. The intricate petal patterns seem to draw the eye inward, creating a sense of mystery that has captivated observers for centuries.

Distinction speaks to the flower's noble history and its association with special occasions. A carnation has long been worn as a boutonniere at weddings and formal events, signifying honor and achievement. The idea of standing out, of being recognized for one's unique qualities, is deeply embedded in this flower's symbolic language.

### Additional Symbolism

- **Endurance:** The carnation's exceptional vase life and hardiness symbolize staying power and resilience in the face of challenges.
- **Maternal Love:** Pink carnations especially represent a mother's eternal love, making them traditional gifts for Mother's Day.
- **Pure Affection:** White carnations often symbolize pure love and good luck, frequently appearing in wedding arrangements.

## Gifting Carnation

### When to Give

- **Birthday celebrations** - Perfect for January birthdays, honoring the recipient's birth month flower
- **Mother's Day** - Pink carnations carry deep maternal symbolism and appreciation
- **Expressions of admiration** - When you want to convey fascination and respect
- **Get-well wishes** - Their long-lasting nature symbolizes hopes for enduring health
- **Anniversaries** - Representing lasting love and continued devotion

### Arrangement Ideas

Carnations pair beautifully with roses for a romantic arrangement, or with baby's breath for a classic, elegant look. For a more modern aesthetic, consider combining carnations with greenery like eucalyptus or ferns. The flowers's varied colors allow for creative expression—try ombré arrangements using different shades of the same color family, or mix complementary colors for visual impact.

When presenting carnations, consider the color's meaning: red for romantic love, pink for gratitude and admiration, white for pure affection and good luck, and yellow for cheerful friendship. Multi-colored bouquets can convey complex messages of affection and appreciation.

### Growing at Home

Carnations thrive in well-drained soil with full sun to partial shade. They prefer cooler temperatures, making them excellent for early spring and fall gardens in most climates. Regular deadheading encourages continued blooming, and they benefit from staking in windy areas due to their slender stems. With proper care, carnation plants can survive for several years, providing blooms season after season—a fitting parallel to the enduring qualities they symbolize.
