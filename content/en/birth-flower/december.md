## About Poinsettia

### Botanical Profile

| Property | Detail |
|----------|--------|
| Scientific Name | Euphorbia pulcherrima |
| Family | Euphorbiaceae (Spurge family) |
| Native Region | Mexico and Central America |
| Blooming Season | Winter (November - February) |
| Colors | Red, white, pink, cream, marbled |

### Description

The poinsettia stands as one of the most recognizable flowering plants in the world, particularly during the winter holiday season. What many people perceive as the flower's brilliant red petals are actually modified leaves called bracts. The true flowers are the small, yellow clusters found at the center of these colorful bracts.

This striking plant can grow as a shrub or small tree, reaching heights of up to 13 feet in its native habitat. The bracts develop their distinctive coloration when exposed to long periods of darkness, typically requiring at least 12 hours of complete darkness each night for several weeks. This photoperiodic response makes the poinsettia naturally suited to bloom during the shortest days of the year.

The plant's dark green leaves provide a dramatic contrast to the vibrant bracts, creating the classic holiday color combination. Modern cultivation has produced varieties in numerous shades beyond the traditional red, including soft pinks, pure whites, speckled patterns, and even orange tones.

### History & Cultural Significance

The poinsettia's journey from wild Mexican shrub to global holiday symbol began with the Aztecs, who called the plant "Cuetlaxochitl." They used the bracts to create red dye and the sap for medicinal purposes to treat fevers. The plant held spiritual significance, with its star-shaped leaf pattern symbolizing purity in Aztec tradition.

The flower's association with Christmas began in 16th-century Mexico, where a legend tells of a young girl named Pepita who had no gift to offer at the Christmas Eve service. An angel guided her to gather weeds from the roadside, and when she placed them at the church altar, they miraculously transformed into brilliant red flowers. This tale earned the poinsettia its Mexican name "Flores de Noche Buena" (Flowers of the Holy Night).

Joel Roberts Poinsett, the first United States Ambassador to Mexico, introduced the plant to the United States in 1825. His passion for botany led him to send specimens to botanical gardens across America, and the plant was eventually named in his honor. By the early 20th century, the Ecke family in California had developed cultivation techniques that transformed the poinsettia into the ubiquitous holiday decoration we know today.

## Gifting Poinsettia

### When to Give

- **Holiday Celebrations** - The classic choice for Christmas gatherings, conveying festive warmth
- **Congratulating Success** - Perfect for celebrating promotions, graduations, or achieved goals
- **Cheering Someone Up** - A thoughtful gift during difficult winter months to bring color and hope
- **Housewarming in Winter** - Welcomes new homeowners with warmth and good wishes

### Arrangement Ideas

Traditional red poinsettias make stunning centerpieces when grouped in varying heights, creating a lush, garden-like display. For a more modern approach, consider white or cream varieties paired with silver accents and eucalyptus for an elegant winter arrangement. Pink poinsettias work beautifully in casual settings and pair well with soft greens and natural wood elements.

When presenting poinsettias as gifts, decorative pot covers in metallic finishes or natural burlap add polish to the presentation. Consider combining a poinsettia with a complementary gift like holiday candles or quality chocolates for a more substantial present. The plants also work wonderfully in mixed arrangements with winter greens, pine cones, and berries.

### Growing at Home

Poinsettias thrive in bright, indirect light and prefer temperatures between 65-75°F (18-24°C). Avoid placing them near drafts, heating vents, or cold windows. Water when the soil surface feels dry, ensuring good drainage to prevent root rot—the most common cause of poinsettia decline.

To encourage your poinsettia to rebloom the following year, reduce watering gradually after the holidays and allow the plant to rest in a cool location. In spring, cut back the stems to about 4 inches and resume regular watering. The key to achieving colorful bracts again lies in providing 12-14 hours of complete darkness each night starting in October, while maintaining normal light during daytime hours.

The birth flower for December is poinsettia, symbolizing success, celebration, and good cheer. This vibrant plant has been associated with winter festivities for centuries, making it a fitting representative for those born in the year's final month.
